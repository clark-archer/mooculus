Fundamental theorem of calculus

After putting in so much work, we finally reap our reward: the fundamental theorem of calculus. The antidifferentiation from Week 10 and the integration from Week 11 combine this week, in a single theorem. You might be wondering:  what is the big deal about the fundamental theorem of calculus? The big deal is that we can evaluate integrals by finding antiderivatives and the latter operation of antidifferentiation is much easier than the Riemann sum business.

What is the fundamental theorem of calculus?

The fundamental theorem of calculus comes in a couple forms. In the  first version of the theorem, we learn that the derivative of the accumulation function is the original function. More explicitly, suppose $$f : [a,b] \to \mathbb{R}$$ is continuous, and define $$F$$ by the rule $$F(x) = \displaystyle\int_a^x f(t) \, dt$$. Then $$F$$ is continuous on $$[a,b]$$ and differentiable on $$(a,b)$$ and $$F'(x) = f(x)$$.

You can play around with  how wiggling the endpoints of integration affects the integral on mooculus. The fundamental theorem is presented on  Page 203 of the Textbook.

How am I supposed to use the fundamental theorem?

In what is often called the  second version of the fundamental theorem, we learn that if, say, $$f$$ is continuous and $$F$$ is an antiderivative of $$f$$, then $$\displaystyle\int_a^b f(x) \, dx = F(b) - F(a)$$. In other words, evaluating an integral amounts to taking the difference of an antiderivative evaluated at the endpoints; you can  practice evaluating integrals using the fundamental theorem on mooculus.

The second version of the fundamental theorem of calculus is presented on  Page 205 of the Textbook.

Additionally, you can see some examples worked out. We can use the fundamental theorem to  evaluate $$\displaystyle\int_0^\pi \sin x \, dx$$ and to  evaluate $$\displaystyle\int_0^1 x^4 \, dx$$

What else can we compute this way?

Integrals are calculating “(signed!) area under the curve” but you can also use them to calculate area between two curves. For instance, we can  compute the area between the graph of $$y = \sqrt{x}$$ and $$y = x^2$$, and we can  compute the area between the graph of $$y = x^2$$ and $$y = 1-x^2$$.

We can also play around with the  accumulation function for a semicircle $$y = \sqrt{1-x^2}$$, which is a great example: we can find the accumulation function by doing some geometry, and also verify that the derivative of the accumulation is $$\sqrt{1-x^2}$$, that is, we can “see” that the fundamental theorem is true in this case.

But why is the fundamental theorem true?

There are a few different perspectives I'd like to present.

We already used the Euler method to numerically approximate antiderivatives. Not coincidentally,  the method of Euler looks a lot like a Riemann sum, which makes sense: the Riemann sum is approximating the accumulation function, which is an antiderivative!

Second, there's an  analogy between summation and integration. We can  use this analogy to compute $$\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^k n^4$$, so if you believe that you can evaluate sums by “antidifferencing” you might believe you can evaluate integrals by antidifferentiating!

Finally, we can  rephrase the fundamental theorem as a physical statement about velocity, which makes it a bit more intuitive: the accumulation function of velocity is computing total displacement, and differentiating displacement is velocity, so it's again, the fundamental theorem!

We'll end the week by  considering $$\displaystyle\frac{d}{dx} \displaystyle\int_x^b f(t) \, dt$$, which gives us a chance to review the fundamental theorem yet again.
